# DGCP™ Case Study #0025

## When Attention Became a Resource

### Why Attention, Trust, and Evidence Follow Different Rules

> *"Attention can be gained quickly. Trust usually cannot. Evidence connects the two."*

## Purpose

This case study examines attention as a limited human resource and explores how it differs from evidence and trust. It observes how reality becomes noticed, communicated, interpreted, documented, and verified before contributing to long-term reputation. The objective is to clarify why visibility may change rapidly while evidence and trust generally develop through repeated observation over time.

---

## Core Observation

Attention, evidence, and trust operate through different patterns.

Attention determines where human focus is directed. It is limited, changes quickly, and may be influenced by novelty, urgency, communication, or changing interests. High attention can create immediate visibility, but visibility alone does not establish accuracy or reliability.

Evidence may be documented, preserved, accumulated, and independently verified. Unlike attention, evidence can remain available after public focus moves elsewhere. Repeated verification connects recorded claims and actions with observable outcomes.

Trust develops more gradually. It may begin through reputation, experience, or communication, but its durability often depends on consistent evidence and repeated verification. Over time, these relationships may contribute to long-term reputation.

---

## Key Points

- Attention is limited and can change rapidly.
- Visibility does not automatically establish trust.
- Evidence can remain after attention moves elsewhere.
- Documentation supports historical continuity.
- Verification connects evidence with observable claims and outcomes.
- Trust develops through consistency observed over time.

---

## Key Insight

Attention may create visibility. Evidence creates continuity. Trust is strengthened when observable evidence remains available and can be repeatedly verified over time.
